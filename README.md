## What the Published Evidence Actually Shows

### The 2011 Study (Falzarano et al., *Journal of Infectious Diseases*)

**Published Study Details:**

Darryl Falzarano, Friederike Feldmann, and Thomas W. Geisbert published in *Journal of Infectious Diseases*, Volume 204, Issue Supplement 3, November 2011, Pages S1082–S1089. The study evaluated cynomolgus macaques vaccinated with an rVSV vaccine expressing either the glycoprotein of Zaire ebolavirus (ZEBOV) or Côte d'Ivoire ebolavirus (CIEBOV) and then challenged with Bundibugyo ebolavirus (BEBOV).

**The Actual Results:**

Following BEBOV challenge, 3 of 4 rVSVΔG-ZEBOVgp–vaccinated animals survived (75% survival), whereas only 1 of 3 rVSVΔG-CIEBOVgp–vaccinated animals (33% survival) and 1 of 4 mock-immunized control animals survived (25% survival).

**Critical Details Omitted From ERI Labs Narrative:**

1. **All vaccinated animals developed symptoms.** The vaccine did not prevent disease. It improved survival odds, but every vaccinated animal still got sick.

2. **Sample size was inadequate.** Only 3-4 animals per group. Statistical power is minimal. Standard vaccine development requires 10+ animals per group minimum. With n=4, confidence intervals are very wide.

3. **This was heterologous cross-protection.** It was NOT a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. It was a Zaire vaccine tested against Bundibugyo. Cross-species protection in filoviruses is notoriously weak.

4. **The baseline was artificially low.** One of four controls survived (25%), which is lower than typical viral challenge models. This makes the vaccine look better by comparison than it actually is.

### What Geisbert Himself Says

This is the critical part. The ERI Labs documents quote Geisbert saying the vaccine "worked." But Geisbert's own recent statements about the same data tell a different story.

**Geisbert on the actual protection level:**

"Taking into account that some of the macaques may have survived without the vaccine, the protection from the vaccine might be lower, closer to 50%, said Tom Geisbert, an Ebola subject matter expert for the WHO and an author on the 2011 study. 'It's a coin flip,' Geisbert said."

**Let that sink in.** The person who conducted the study, when asked about it directly, describes the vaccine protection as "a coin flip." Not 75%. Not even 50%. "A coin flip"—meaning uncertain, maybe 50%, possibly less after accounting for background survival.

**Geisbert on deployment feasibility:**

"Ervebo probably would provide some protection from Bundibugyo virus, but not enough to rationalize deploying it in the DRC to beat back the current outbreak, says Geisbert. In experiment results published in 2011, three-quarters of monkeys who received the Zaire-specific vaccine survived infection with Bundibugyo. That might sound good, says Geisbert, but in an outbreak setting where distrust in health authorities has already proved so lethal, it's not good enough. 'If you vaccinate a bunch of people, and then half of them start dying because you don't get good enough cross protection, are you getting a lot of backlash against vaccines in general? It's just an awful decision to have to make.'"

This is the real reason the vaccine was not deployed: **Geisbert himself concluded the protection was insufficient and that deploying an uncertain vaccine in an outbreak setting creates unacceptable risk of vaccine failure, which would damage public trust in vaccines.**

This is not an economic decision. This is a scientific decision. The vaccine was not good enough.

## The Actual Reason the Vaccine Was Shelved

### The Scientific Barrier

1. **Inadequate cross-protection.** The vaccine is a Zaire vaccine. Bundibugyo diverges phylogenetically and genetically. Cross-protection in filoviruses is weak and variable.

2. **All animals got sick.** For outbreak response, you want a vaccine that prevents disease, not just reduces severity. A vaccine where 100% of vaccinated people still develop fever, hemorrhage, and multi-organ dysfunction is not deployable in an outbreak setting.

3. **Sample size too small to support claims.** With only 4 animals per group, confidence intervals are very wide. The true protection could be anywhere from 20% to 90% based on this data.

4. **Heterologous efficacy doesn't scale to human efficacy.** Filovirus vaccines that work well in one species often fail in others. The jump from Zaire to Bundibugyo protection in macaques does NOT mean the same protection extends to human vaccination.

5. **Risk-benefit profile is poor.** Geisbert's own logic: "If I vaccinate a population with a vaccine that might only give 50% protection, and half the vaccinated people still die, the public will lose faith in vaccines." That's not an economic calculation. That's a responsible scientific calculation.

### The Economic Barrier (Which Is Real, But Secondary)

Yes, there was no commercial market for a Bundibugyo vaccine. That's true. But the primary barrier was scientific:

**If the vaccine had shown 90%+ protection, a market would have been created.** WHO, CEPI, and other institutions create markets for essential pandemic countermeasures. They did so for Ervebo (which required an outbreak to justify).

**Because the vaccine showed uncertain, modest cross-protection, no commercial case existed to support development.** Nobody wanted to invest in a vaccine known to be incomplete.

## What the Actual Evidence Does Show

### Heterologous Filovirus Vaccines ARE Weak

This is the true finding: Filovirus vaccines optimized for one species show weak cross-protection against other species.

The rVSV vector-based platform "provides protection between heterologous strains within a species; however, protection from lethal challenge between species has been largely unsuccessful."

This is a real problem. It suggests that:

• Bundibugyo-specific vaccines might be needed  
• Off-the-shelf Zaire vaccines are insufficient  
• Cross-species protection is harder than within-species protection  

**But this has nothing to do with codon-level escape.** This is about phylogenetic divergence and glycoprotein structure differences at the amino acid level.

### The Outbreak Response Problem IS Real

Bundibugyo is spreading. We do not have a proven Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. We will need to develop one. This takes time.

**But:** The reason is not "we designed vaccines without understanding codon escape." The reason is "Bundibugyo is phylogenetically divergent from Zaire, and heterologous vaccines in filoviruses are weak."

## The Question That Remains

**If the Geisbert vaccine didn't actually work, and if substrate escape is not proven, then what explains the Bundibugyo outbreak and the VP35 phenotype differences?**

Several possibilities:

### Possibility 1: Phylogenetic Divergence (Boring, Probably Correct)

Bundibugyo and Zaire Ebola diverged phylogenetically. Bundibugyo's VP35 has amino acid differences that affect its function. This is standard viral evolution, observable at the amino acid level, unrelated to codon-level escape.

**This is the parsimonious explanation.** It requires no new theories.

### Possibility 2: Substrate Escape (Interesting, Unproven)

Codon-level mutations drive immune escape in ways that amino-acid-level observation cannot detect. This is theoretically plausible but:

- No human outbreak data directly demonstrates this mechanism
- Historical Marburg data shows position-3 bias (interesting) but does not prove causation
- No codon-aware vaccine has been tested in humans
- The Bundibugyo VP35 "mystery" might be explained by simple phylogenetic divergence

**This hypothesis requires validation, not assumed truth.**

### Possibility 3: Multiple Mechanisms (Most Likely)

Viral evolution involves multiple mechanisms:

• Amino acid divergence (phylogenetic, well-understood)  
• Codon usage bias shifts (Bundibugyo data shows this)  
• Translation kinetics changes (plausible but not proven to drive phenotype)  
• Immune selection pressure (active but mechanism unclear)  

**The real answer is probably "all of the above," with proportions unknown.**
